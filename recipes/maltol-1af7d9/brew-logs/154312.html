Brewed by jll23

Brewing Notes

Mashing with a little over 1.75 gallons. ~.5 of that is distilled water, the rest is tap water that I've added Campden to.

Will "sparge" with a little over 1.5 gallons of campden-treated tap water.

This yeast seemingly likes higher temperatures, and as luck would have it tomorrow starts a stretch of 80F+ weather.

My grains this time all come from North America. The pale and pils are Rahr; the 2-row is Briess. I tend to use a lot of German stuff (and Belgian where appropriate), but since this was a less specifically malt-focused beer I decided to use what I'm sure are still good quality domestic malts.

Cooled the wort down to around 80F and pitched the yeast. Draped a blanket around it and put it into a closet that is currently in the lower 70's. I assume that the temperature will rise with fermentation, but hopefully not much over 10 degrees or so. Seems like that will be enough of a cushion, but I'll keep a close eye on it.

Shooting low in terms of carbonation with this beer. It'll still be a "modern" level, but I want to try to be more faithful to a beer style that often has few if any bubbles.

Taste

Wort sample tastes sweet and of light malt. The bitterness is way in the background. Just keeping it from tasting like sugar water. I rarely make a beer this light (at least without wheat in it), so having such simple, light malt tastes gives me pause. There's really no reason that this shouldn't turn out, though. The wort tastes pretty much as I'd expect. Now it's up to the yeast.

Bottling sample: Pretty light yellow-gold color. Clearer than I'd imagine at this stage. Crazy amount of strange fruit aromas. It's like Belgian-level amounts of esters, just very different ones. I'm loathe to try and pick out individual fruit aromas for fear of reducing this very unique experience to just that. Malt-wise, there is a touch of graininess maybe from the Pils or the pale. It's not much flavor, but it goes really well with the fruit. Just adds a subtle extra dimension to the slightly otherworldly aromas. There is also a little bit of bitterness in this, and you can certainly tell hops are present, even if they aren't providing aroma per se. They're definitely in the background, but are doing some general counterbalancing to the sweet fruit aromas. This is weird, but I would totally drink this beer uncarbonated (which is how they usually are enjoyed in their native environment). The lightness of the fruitiness really is unique, and it's almost as if it doesn't need the carbonation to bring those volatile elements out. They're already in the forefront. Let's hope I didn't mess up a good thing by adding priming sugar to it.

Opening the first bottle, about a week after it was put in it. It's a light, blondish color. The beer was pretty clear when I bottled it, but it seems to have gotten a bit hazier, perhaps from bottle conditioning. It already has a bit of carbonation. Again, I didn't add a lot of sugar, so it should be low-moderate at best. Based on the carbonation level in this beer, it might have converted the majority already. The standout thing about this beverage is, of course, the most intense tropical fruit flavors I have ever smelled in a beer. And, we know that's not coming from hops based on my very conservative usage of them. I definitely get pineapple and mango esters. There's a touch of grain flavor in there, but I don't think it's going to persist very long as the beer ages. I can tell there is hop bitterness in there, but it's quite mild. Whatever this is, it's already pretty drinkable. Just a very light fruity beer, with possibly more strength than one might imagine (though it is by no means very strong).

About 3 weeks in the bottle. Light, somewhat sweet, with fruity aromas. I feel like the fruit esters keep fading, which is somewhat disappointing since that is the sole focus of the beer. I continue to question my water, since this and other ones occasionally make my teeth feel a little weird. Anyway, this beer is like a belgian blond, but not quite. It's got that strength plus the light malt and then some interesting light fruit.

About 6 weeks after bottling. Let not the taste timestamp check ins mislead you, I have been regularly tasting this beer between the last entry and now (something that typically happens with most of the brews). This is a pretty lovely, sweet-ish ester-full light-colored ale of moderate strength. It has a good, slightly golden, yellow color. The yeast is and will always be the star. The malt is surprisingly nice, given the unintended complexity of the grain bill. The hops have all but faded into the background as they play their structural role only. This could really be the beginning of a lot of new interesting beers, but in its simplicity this becomes pretty great. It is like a different kind of Belgian beer (yes, Norwegian). A blond, I think I said previously, and those words are still true. I chose what I was told were neutral magnum hops for bittering, but I've brewed enough to know that nothing is neutral. They have finally sort of fallen away, but in general even just their bitterness added a mild herbal quality to the young beer. I think we can do better than that, and I would basically suggest with the next brewing of this recipe that we take a page from Belgian beers and use those hops. There are so many similarities in the way the esters play out to Belgian beers and even though it might seem odd (especially with the lesser phenolic component) that is the best analog we currently have. These are far more interesting/exotic fruit esters that I find in this beer, but the path remains remarkably the same. Use EKG's, or Spalt, or whatever else you would for Belgians or fruity European ales. These are great, complex works, and you should use subtle (but magnificent) hops that reflect that. There are some sulfurous barnyard notes to this beer. I don't know if that is because I fermented it warm or if I packaged it in bottles that had previously housed a French Saison fermented beer (they sometimes produce a non-wild yeast residue in the bottle necks that I have attempted to remove but sometimes fail to). I don't take enough notes on that kind of thing to determine it, but regardless, it's not unpleasant. It makes it seem more like a Belgian beer, at any rate. This is the good shit. This is where you take a risk on something unknown and not only learn a lot about flavors but produce an extremely delicious batch in the process. Yeah, I'd use this yeast again, and I'd certainly brew something (probably more simple) like this recipe again.

05/24/2018 1:23am

Fermentation has already begun. Pretty vigorous airlock activity. A little bit of foam is forming on top. My temp strip only goes up to 78, but I'm currently assuming it's in the 80's. So far so good.

05/24/2018 2:37pm

There's still some weird looking foam on top of the beer, but the bubbling has already slowed down some. Still going steadily, but nowhere near as fast as it was last night. Maybe the yeast already tore through most of the sugars? I've heard that kveik from this location is supposed to work fast.
05/24/2018 8:10pm

Bubbling has slowed down to a crawl. The layer of krausen that remains on top isn't thick, but it looks really goopy. It just generally looks kind of strange. The temperature still seems to be in the 80's, I'm guessing. It hasn't fully crashed yet, at any rate.

05/25/2018 2:40am

Temp strip is starting to read again, which means the temperature has dipped down under 80F. Still has a thin layer of brownish krausen on it, which is holding bubbles, almost like a pellicle (but not quite). Pretty odd. Hopefully that will crash over the next week.
