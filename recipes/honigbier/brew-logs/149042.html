Brewed by jll23

On April 21, 2017

Brewing Notes

Mashing with about 1.8 gallons of water, which is made up of campden treated tap water and about a half gallon or so of distilled water.

Sparging with about 1.75 gallons of campden treated tap water.

Honey is grocery store bought "wildflower" variety.

Cooled the wort down to 65 and pitched it on the yeast cake. Hoping to keep things on the cool side for what I imagine with be a pretty quick fermentation. The fact that it is on the cool side, at least provides me with some insurance against vigorous exothermic fermentation.

Taste

At bottling, it smells like a malty German beer, but the honey is pretty pronounced in the taste. Color is kind in the Oktoberfest neighborhood. There's some spice from the booze. So far it seems like there's enough bitterness (though it is behind the scenes) to keep it from being cloying. That, and the fact that it's actually a fairly dry beer. Some fermented honey harshness on the end that I'm hoping will mellow with age.

I opened one of these after a week in the bottle, as is not unusual for me these days. I'm pretty impressed so far. Decent carbonation and foam, considering how early it is in the bottle conditioning phase, and the amount of honey I used. The flavors are super-malty and therefore come off as sweet, even though it's not actually terribly sweet. I don't find it hard to drink, in fact it's quite the opposite. The honey and the malt flavors go together quite well, and the yeast flavors (understated as they are with this strain) create a nice vehicle. There's some nice alcohol warmth, but it's relatively restrained, considering the strength of the beer and the portion that's comprised of simpler sugars. Perhaps most surprisingly, there's some really great hop flavors/aromas in this, coming from the perle. Fruity and green. Mild citrus rind? A little tropical. The beer still finishes with kind of a bitter roughness, which I continue to attribute to the honey. It already seems more harmonious than it was at bottling, so I expect it will continue to age well. Seems like it's going to be a really good beer.

I've got to stop drinking this beer and let it age some, but it's pretty damn pleasant, even young. Yeah, the finish is still a bit weird, but more than anything the beer tastes like wort. It's got the nice light hop notes and then the honey reinforces all the sweet malt flavors. I imagine it'll be even better with time, but I've got to let it get there.

After a month in bottling, it's a nice reddish/amber beer. She's on the sweet side, but really nice and German. Plus honey. And a lot of fruity hop flavor from the perle hops. It's a solid, strong beer. Still, honey ferments on a different schedule and the finish remains particular. This leads to booze aromas/flavors, but they go really well with the hops and all the malt. Yeast is a nice mild way to deliver all of this. Only going to get milder, but it's already pretty good. Honey-booze, German +hops. That's where this one is at.

Over two months in bottle. Just a couple of things. First, the honey seems to have been slowly fermenting in the bottle, which is interesting. It seems to be drying things out a little and definitely increasing the carbonation. Previous comments apply, but the contours of the beer are changing. With the increase in dryness and carbonation, it takes on almost a more Belgian-y character, but without most of the esters. You have some honey flavors and then whatever German yeast esters (plus the diminishing, but still present, malt flavors and hop inputs). Second, the bubbles on this one seem really big. Kind of off-white in color. They don't necessarily last super long, but they are way larger than what I think of Belgian beer having (not that that was what I initially imagine comparing this too, it's just what I think is a more apt criterion at this point). Continues to be really interesting. Dryer and boozier.

Trying one of the last few bottles of these almost 6 months after bottling. Kind of hazy, amber-red in color. It's a sweet beer, with honey and malt flavors. The fact that the honey is fermenting makes it less sweet overall, but the impression of sweetness combined with the malt leaves that general impression. Some fruit flavor, largely from the hops (which are understated, though noticeable) and maybe the yeast. A little bit of spice from the honey. That kind of doubles as a graininess, too, all of which seems very welcome in a German beer. So, we have a strong, sweet-ish beer with hints of fruit and malt, made all the more complex with the honey flavors and the glorious German yeast. Nice, pretty easy drinking strong German ale. Nothing very extreme in terms of flavor, but pleasant and interesting.

Notes

Date Note
04/22/2017 2:43am

Bubbling has begun. There's a slight white cloudiness gathering at the top of the fermentor. Pretty quick turnaround, as expected with a yeast cake this size (at already waiting at fermentation temps).

Not a lot of headspace on this one, but based on the three other beers I've made with this yeast it hopefully won't be a problem.

04/22/2017 1:57pm

Vigorous fermentation began last night and this morning it has kicked into yet another gear. The beer had been around 68F, but now is threatening to climb even higher. Airlock is so busy that small amounts of water are coming out the top. The yeast did eventually create about an inch of foamy krausen on top of the beer, but so far is resisting the urge to completely fill the carboy. Not a lot of room to go, still, but holding steady at this point.
04/23/2017 3:17am

I left the windows open today as a way of moderating the temperature. This meant that the ambient temperature of the apartment gradually fell. In carboy terms, the temperature fell to somewhere in the 66-67F range. Air lock activity has been pretty constant throughout the process. Since I don't want the beer to cool down much beyond that, I've closed all the windows at the set the thermostat for a minimum 65F.

04/23/2017 5:36pm

Air lock still bubbling along. It's no longer going at a breakneck pace, but still pretty often. Foam has receded noticeably. Temp around 67F.
04/26/2017 6:02pm

The temperature outside has spiked again in the last few days and it's been in the mid-high 70's. I've kept the windows closed so as to keep it as cool as possible inside, but even then the apartment has gotten into the low 70's on occasion. I'm basically trusting that with all the yeast in the fermentor and the really vigorous fermentation that the beer is mostly done already and that elevated temperatures won't have much impact on the outcome. I'll know for sure in another week or so.
