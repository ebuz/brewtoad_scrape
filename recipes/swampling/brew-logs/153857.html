Brewed by jll23

Brewing Notes

New apartment, new stove. This is my inaugural run in these conditions, so it should be interesting.

Mashing with a little over a gallon of water that is primarily campden-treated tap water with less than .25 gallons of distilled water.

Will sparge with a little over 3 gallons of campden-treated tap water.

Pils is Dingemans, Munich and wheat are Weyermann (Barke in the case of the Munich).

Cooled the wort down to about 67F and pitched a full smack pack (~3 weeks old). Ambient temperature is somewhere around 69F, so I'd be happy if it gets up to the low 70's.

Managed to hit my target gravity, which is not a bad way to start in a new kitchen.

Also, really liked the sterling aroma, so I tossed a couple partial pellets in during cooling phase.

Having just bottled this, I can say that the French Saison yeast is just as crazy as I remember. It took the beer down to almost zero.

I went somewhat conservative on the priming sugar, just because I wasn't positive the fermentation would be complete after a little over a week. Spoiler: it's about as complete as is hydrometrically possible. I'm still hoping the carbonation will be only moderate, given how dry this finished.

Taste

My OG sample of this beer is kind of in that straw color range. There's a little bit of breadiness to it, and it also has a fair bit of bitterness. I'm hoping this will just make the end product crisp. It'll probably depend on how low the beer finishes.

At bottling, the sample is a cloudy yellow. It has fruity notes and flowery aromas from the yeast and hop combos. Drinks nice and light, but there is a hint of graininess from the wheat and a little bit of sweet malt from the Munich. Slightly bitter at the end, but mostly dry. I think this is going to be a nice spring beer.

Trying one about a week after bottling. Reasonable carbonation already. Nice yellow/light gold color. Surprisingly clear for this yeast. It has a very sweet fruity aroma to it. Most of that is from the hops, I think, but there are some classic saison characteristics and a little funk in the nose. It's light and crisp, though you can taste the malt additions as well (wheat and Munich). The finish is pretty dry, with bitterness lingering even after the beer is gone. I'm starting to wonder if that's not something with the new apartment, like the water. There was something similar with the Antwerpen Biscuit beer I just bottled. Maybe like minerals or metals. It's still pretty early to make that call, but I'm noticing something in these first few that are affecting the perceived bitterness in a way I'm not sure I like. Might plan to use more bottled water in my next batch.

Trying another one of these about two weeks after bottling (maybe a little less). Again has okay carbonation. Not sparkling or anything like, that, but a little foam remains well after pouring. It smells really nice. Fruity and bready. There are almost some fresh flowery notes in there too. I think the hop flavor/aroma is a little less noticeable than it used to be. The grain taste in this one is really nice. It's got a nice sweet/mild wheatiness to it. Pretty excellent. The beer still finishes a little dry, but it has a nice body to it. I can tell it's a smaller beer, but the flavors go a long way to making it feel like I'm not missing much when I drink this instead of something bigger. Nice, easy drinking farmhouse beer. Dry, decently bitter, with a hint of hops, but the star for me is the grain taste. Munich + Wheat = nice sweet wheat flavor, but I would be remiss to ignore Dingemann's base malt which is far sweeter tasting than, say, German pils. Really good and simple malt combo for this sort of beer. I will also say that after a little more aging, I'm not really noticing any weird flavors like the ones I thought I maybe tasted closer to bottling. Things have melded well. The funk has blended in to the rest too. All in all, this is about perfect for late spring/early summer. Refreshing and aromatic, reminding me of the season.

A few more days have past. The carbonation is actually really nice for what this is supposed to be. Tiny shimmering bubbles, perfect for a Belgian Farmhouse beer. The hops are already fading a little bit on this one. It smells sort of lemony in general, but some of that is from the yeast esters. Still really nice round bready flavors, with a hint of herbal hop aroma on top. Again a really nice experience, albeit kind of a unique one. This yeast gets a lot of flack, and I understand that it's very different from the other main saison strains that are available. The fruitiness of this one will probably never compete with the Belgian Saison or Farmhouse strain, but the trade off is that beers made with this variety will be very different. The texture is amazing, that's often overlooked. It's a totally different kind of drinking experience because of that alone. When you add in the very restrained esters and the (often quite subtle) phenolics, it seems to me that what you get is a totally different sort of beer. It's not going to produce Saison DuPont, but it's not really supposed to. Its weirdness can make it a little difficult to know what to do with (particularly if you're envisioning a more traditional Saison), but if you can play to its strengths (perhaps doing something slightly maltier to balance out its crazy attenuation) you'll get crazy interesting results. That has been one of my biggest takeaways from using it again after a number of years. It goes really well with wheat and hops.

I killed off Swampling after a little less than three months. It inaugurated the new place, reintroduced me to the 3711 (and saison brewing in general), and let me give Sterling hops another try. This final sample has been in the fridge for a while and it's clear with a lovely Belgian small bubble head on it. Shit is beautiful. Aroma and taste, she's a swampy/herbal beer. Most notably, while I did like the hops when they were very young, that quickly changed. They're intense and not in a good way. They are on brand with a beer called Swampling, but I don't otherwise love them much. The other aspects of this beer are quite nice. The bubbles are nice and shimmery, the remaining malt flavors are perfectly appropriate for this kind of beer, and the yeast, while quite subtle does the job. Looking back, though this is a far better executed beer, I remember why I didn't love Swamp King. I will say a lot of that is the hops, even if there is conceptual synergy with their particular flavor profile. In general, the combination could be much improved. There are a bunch more hops of similar or adjacent provenance that I like so much more. I only want to drink so much swamp water.
