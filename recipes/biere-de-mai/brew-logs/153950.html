Brewing Notes

Mashing with about 2 gallons, ~a third of a gallon of that is distilled, the rest is tap water with a little campden sprinkled in.

Will "sparge" with about 1.25 gallons. Not sure how great efficiency will be with this much grain. Sadly, the stove doesn't boil vigorously enough for me to risk more water on the front end.

Pils this time is from Avangard. Munich and Vienna are Weyermann Barke.

Threw one hop pellet in during cool down.

Close to target gravity.

Cooled the wort down to about 66F and pitched it on top of Swampling's cake. Tried to make a closet as dark as possible, put a fan going in there and placed the fermentor inside. We'll see how this goes.

Anticipating a smaller yield this time. The yeast cake is pretty huge. Also, the beer is still quite cloudy, but it's been two weeks and this strain tends to be a fast fermenter. I'm just hoping that the cloudiness doesn't imply something different, like that it's still working away on the longer chains I created from higher mashing temperatures. That would suck.

Credit where credit is due: I tried to outsmart the yeast by mashing at a higher temperature. The yeast easily overcame that apparently feeble attempt. Not sure what else I could have done. Mash at 160? At any rate, the outcome gives the impression of sweetness and this yeast always produces great texture/body. Mashing doesn't seem to matter much, but that's okay.

At bottling, the temperature is around 74F. Went on the lower side of priming sugar.

Taste

At bottling, this beer is a hazy light orange/almost peachy color. Smells fantastically of fruits and sweet bready malts. The yeast took it down to almost nothing again, so it has the flavors of sweet malts, but ultimately isn't. There's a little bitterness to balance all the malt, but it really only does that. The hop aroma is already melding with the esters and malt. I'd say that this one has a little bit more in terms of phenolics than the last one, but they're pretty mild yet. They just do some rounding out of what would otherwise be a pretty malty, fruity beer. I guess it has even more of a saison flavor to it as a result. This turned out pretty strong what with the super-attentuation, but there's very little higher alcohol heat that I'm getting. Wasn't sure what I was going to end up with, and I did envision a slightly sweeter beer. That said, this seems pretty damn delicious so far.

Broke down and tried one a little less than a week after bottling. Pretty okay carbonation already. Still a lot cloudier than Swampling ever was. This one clearly has more malt aroma and taste in it. I can definitely smell the slight dry, grainy spice of the Vienna malt. It goes a long way to making it taste more "German." I think there's a little bit more yeast esters in this one. Makes it slightly fruity and citrusy. The aroma of this beer is really complex. It tastes a little spicy (almost clove-like), but also quite sweet and malty. Will be really interesting to see how it ages, but still looking like something good and interesting so far. A lot stronger than Swampling, obviously, but the strength only adds to the complexity.

Still about a week out from bottling. I am struck this time by the hoppiness of the beer, paired with the yeast and then the malt. These Sterling hops are noble-ish, but a bit more intense than that in terms of aroma. That is part of the idea in a swamp-themed beer this is following on the heels of (see Swampling). It definitely is playing out on the hoppy side of this one, even though it's supposed to be on the maltier side (that said, I only used base malts). Some of that is the lack of residual sugars with the yeast. I could have hopped more conservatively as well, but I liked the aroma of this variety. Herbal and fruity. Got some funk from the yeast that blends well with the slightly more American notes. There's a whole thing to get into in terms of what is associated (rightly or wrongly, with variation, as the case may be) with American hops, but not tonight. These are more intense noble-ish hops, with additional fruit and funk. A lot of it's pretty nice. This is a pretty golden beer. This one seems to have clarified some, finally. Moderate carbonation, but decent retention. There's a lazy layer floating on top, largely ignoring my swirls. Still good, I just hope I haven't complicated the flavors too much by packing too many elements into this one.

I've tried a number of these by now, but it's a little under a month in bottle for this particular taste. The malts smell reminiscent of German beers, but the esters and phenolics are clearly Belgian. Definitely taste the dryness/toastiness from the vienna. This beer does have a more complex saison aroma than Swampling, its predecessor. I'd say that this one seems to have improved over the month in bottling. It's become more refined. The hop aroma has tempered some, and the spice has really become a nice complex through-line. Importantly, again, the French saison yeast adds lots of really distinct dimensions to it. I'm liking it quite a bit. It's got a pretty good balance of sweet malts/mild fruit esters and dryness, enhanced by the spice.

Having another one of these, maybe a day after the last one. A few things. First, this was perhaps the riskiest beer flavor combination I've attempted at the new place. In the past, it has been difficult for me to balance sweet-ish germanic base malts with fruity and spicy farmhouse yeasts. You add hops to that, and there's just so much happening. A lot can go wrong. The idea always sounds good, but the reality is often far muddier and less spectacular. Stylistically, the continental cross-pollination that is a Biere de Mai makes no sense. And yet, it somehow works in the glass. This is the strongest beer I've made here, and it has the most distinct flavor combinations within it. Oh, and it attenuated down to almost nothing. It could have easily been a disaster (or sub optimal outcome) at any number of crucial junctures, but the results are surprisingly unique, interesting, and downright good. I'm going to credit a lot of the success here to Vienna malt, which doubled down on the dryness and spiciness of the yeast. There were sweeter malts in there, sure, but they were tempered by the toastiness of the Vienna. The sterling hops so far are working impeccably well with all the other noise that's carrying on. Weird, and very herbal on top of their fruitiness, but again it somehow works. Anyway, that's the story of the beer. A little bit more higher alcohols due to the strength, I think, but they actually work out well with the yeast and other factors in this unlikely beer.

About six weeks in the bottle now, and it's just really sounded out well. It's got a really nice (somewhat understated) farmhouse quality, complete with a little funk. There's some definitively Germanic malt. The vienna and the pils really go well in this. What's left of the hops are pretty good too. I'll say this about Sterling hops: They're kind of like noble hops in that they're definitely not your standard American C or crazy fruity hops. At the same time, they have nowhere near as mild and refined hop flavor or bitterness as the real thing. So much more herbal and flowery. A lot of noble-ish hybrids have different high notes from their crossbreeding and this remains true, it's just not really fruity at all to my taste. It tastes like the other parts of the plant, and a lot of those really delicate aspects seem to fade pretty quickly. Again, I don't dislike it in this context. I have wondered about it in others (especially when the herbal elements fade), but mostly I wonder if the beer would be better with EKG's or Spalt, with their different, but legitimately timeless hop flavors. Anyway, what is here really works. This is nearly 8% but drinks much easier than that and offers so much complexity.

I'm finishing this off after a bit more than three months in the bottle. This last survivor was a .750 bomber, and had spent significant time conditioning in the fridge. Based on previous experiences with this batch and more generally with the yeast, I expected unruly over-carbonation. Pleasantly, that was not the case. The first half of the pour was crystal clear, while still being accompanied by tons of micro-bubbles. The beer continues to mellow as it ages. Some of the malts have gotten grainy-er. Maybe it's the age, maybe it's the vienna, I don't know. I thought some of it might have to do with the diminished hops, and while they are still very diminished, I'm not sure how much else they are to blame for. This beer is still sort of sweetly malty, under all the hop remains. The esters are minimal, but the phenolic notes have literally haunted any other beer I brewed in these same bottles. I don't want to make this claim in the same panicked tones that people talked about brett infections previously, but basically this tweener strain won't die and can easily influence subsequent bottlings. It's more complicated than that, in my case, and I'm dealing with a few other yeast strains whose particular fermentation characteristics I'm just starting to understand. Still, this one has always been a beast and I wouldn't want to take anything away from it. Phenol king, funky extreme fermenter. I tip my metaphorical hat to you, even while I won't be subjecting my system to your lingering presence anytime soon. Sleep well you spicy S. O. B..
