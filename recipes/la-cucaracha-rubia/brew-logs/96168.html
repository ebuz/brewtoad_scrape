La Cucaracha Rubia

an All Grain Blonde Ale by Kyle Kestell

  • Ingredients
  • 1 Brew Logs
  • 2 Variants

Print
Export BeerXML

Brewed by Kyle Kestell

On June 10, 2012

Brewing Notes

8:09 AM

Brewing this today using a no-sparge mash. The plan is to mash 10.25lbs of grain with 8gal of water at 152°F for 60 minutes. This is a crazy-thin mash at 3.2 qt/lbs, but I've read reports of people having good success with mashes as thin as 3.5 qt/lbs, so I'm not too concerned.

I've always read that a thicker mash will yield more unfermentable sugars, but Palmer says that mash thickness is much less important than temperature in this regard--something I imagine is true for the ranges of temperatures and mash thickness that we're likely to encounter for most OGs and batch sizes.

Since I don't know exactly what kind of efficiency to expect from this kind of mash, so I've assumed 65%, and have designed the recipe so the OG sits at the high end of the range. I've got a few pounds of DME ready too, just in case I'm still a few points short. If I'm high, I'll add some water and adjust the hop schedule accordingly.

9:09 AM

Took 1:08 to heat the mash water to 158°F. Mash tun is preheated. Doughing-in!

9:41 AM

Of course this is obvious in retrospect, but my strike water temperature dropped a lot less when I doughed-in than I expected it would (due to how thin my mash is). Mash temp was almost exactly what my strike temp was (just a hair shy of 160°F). Got it down to about 155°F with some ice. Rolling with it. Mash on!

11:03 AM

Collected about 6.4gal runnings at 1.039 and transfered to the boil kettle.

11:39 AM

Boil! Added 1/2 oz Cascade.

11:59 AM

Added 1 whirlflock tablet.

12:24 PM

Added 1/2 oz Cascade and 1/2 tsp yeast nutrient.

12:30 PM

Disaster strikes! New sink with nonstandard threads. Can't attach the wort chiller. So, change of plans. Transferring the wort to a bucket and pitching the yeast tomorrow morning. I think it'll be fine. If not, I'll write the batch off as a trial run doing no-sparge in the new space. Worst case, it gives me an excuse to brew again sooner.

7:11 AM

The bucket feels cool to the touch, but I haven't opened it yet for fear of contamination. Over lunch today, I'm going to rack to a glass carboy, aerate, and pitch the yeast.

12:17 PM

The yeast is pitched. Now we wait. The wort still looked/smelled great when I transferred it into the carboy, and letting it sit overnight had the added benefit of letting a good inch or so of trub settle to the bottom of the bucket. Of course, I lost some volume transferring; I'd estimate that I now have been 4 and 4.5 galons of wort. C'est la vie!

I ended up with an OG of 1.046, with about 4.8gal of wort. I'll have to run the numbers later, but I estimate I got about 55-60% efficiency.

A couple observations:

  • Because of how thin the mash is, I had much less of a temperature drop when doughing-in than I expected. In the future, I'll expect to only lose a degree or two.

  • Because of how full my mash tun was (only three or four inches from the top), I lost a lot less temperature over my 60 min mash than I usually do. Only three degrees or so, and that was after I opened the tun a few times to stir the mash and take gravity readings.

  • It's cool to be able to take a quick refractometer reading when I stir the mash, knowing that whatever I measure in the tun is what I'll end up with in my kettle. This makes it dead simple to know when the mash is done, and gives me some advanced notice in the event my SG is low, so I can measure out a corrective amount of malt extract while I lauter.

7/4/2012

Bottling day. Fermented way down to 1.008. That puts it at about 4.8% ABV. This might just be one of the best batches of beer I've made. It's crystal clear and fermented really clean. Here's a pic of the sample I took before I bottled it.
