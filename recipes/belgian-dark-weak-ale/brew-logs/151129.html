an All Grain Belgian Specialty Ale by jll23

Brewed by jll23

Brewing Notes

Mashing with about 1.33 gallons. ~.25 of that is distilled water and the rest is campden-treated tap water.

Will rinse with about 2 gallons of campden-treated tap water.

Might add a few rice hulls at the end of the mash. I'm not using much oats, but I figure I have the hulls anyway and it couldn't hurt.

I should note that I lost a little grain at the store while trying to transfer it from the mill to a plastic bag. Not very concerned about it. This is supposed to be a small beer. Lately I've been overachieving in the efficiency department, so this might just help hedge that a bit.

Using legitimate Styrian Goldings in this one for the first time in a couple years. They had the real thing (and not one of the hybrids) at the store and, for what I'm doing, 4% is enough this time. I just used English Fuggles last week, so I'm curious how they'll compare.

Tossed in a couple of very tiny pellet shards with under 15 minutes to go in the boil. Doubt they'll do much, but we'll see.

Cooled the wort down to under 73F and pitched most of the yeast pack (it was made in late August, so there should be plenty of yeast to ferment this small beer). 3rd time in a row where the chiller struggled to get things cooler than the low-mid 70's. Today it was actually in the mid 70's outside, but I still don't expect my tap water to be the exact temperature of external air. It's a weird situation.

The gravity sample of wort tasted quite sweet and malty. Bready, with honey flavors. Also notes of almost burnt sugar coming from the darker malts. Noticeable bitterness at the end, but nothing too crazy. It all points to a good outcome so far.

Managed to mildly overshoot target OG on this one. Still right in the range of where I want it. Now we'll see how dry this yeast get it. Here's hoping not too low.

Taste

At bottling, it tastes pretty malty, but also fairly dry and acidic. Interesting so far. Decently dark in color. Smells great.

Sneaking a taste after a little over a week in the bottle. I expect it will get more carbonated with time, but I'm already impressed with what is a fairly luxurious head on this beer. I'm going to credit the oats for that one. The bubbles are impressively large, not tiny and sparkling like some very carbonated farm house beers I've made in the past. It's a light reddish-brown color. Dark enough to fit the name, but not super brown. The beer is fruity and malty. Drinks fairly dry considering all that. It still tastes pretty young, so it'll be interesting to see how things age. There's sweetness from the cara-munich. The fruit flavors are a combination of the yeast and maybe the special b. There's a bit of chocolate bread in there too. The finish is interesting and I chalk up the sort of acidity it brings to the yeast. Very different for a beer with these flavor combinations. All in all, nothing too strong flavor-wise. A bunch of malt layers on top of a seemingly unique Belgian yeast. Hop flavor exists very mildly in the nose, but it's another kind of sweet/herbal flavor. A bit grainy tasting at this point. I wonder how much of that is from the oats. I'm still trying to isolate what they bring to the flavor.

About 6 weeks in the bottle. Still has crazy head and carbonation, at least at first (large, soapy bubbles). Still has a very strange grain flavor, probably from the oats. Yeah, this yeast just has a unique finish to it. What's new recently is that there's some funk developing in there. It's not super strong, but a little sulfurous barnyard note is in there. It actually goes pretty well with all the other elements. Kind of brings another level of complexity, but also sort of a unifying influence to this beer. Finally seems a bit more Belgian. Anyway, this is a decent, odd beer. It probably could have benefited from more dark malt (somehow). Those flavors are pretty subdued in conjunction with this very different yeast.

It's 2+ months in the bottle. The head is pretty spectacular, but somehow doesn't seem too carbonated to me at the moment. It's more refined as opposed to having a carbonic bite/thinness. I feel like the malt flavors have mellowed, and I smell more herbal notes from the Styrian Goldings (this was the first beer in a while where I used them). I really wasn't sure if I liked the when the beer was young, but they have aged into something nice. Slightly spicy, but overall a nice mild flavor that blends well with the rest of the beer. On paper this beer has a lot of malt flavors going for it, but in reality other than the oat-grain notes (which have thankfully gotten subtler), nothing intense stands out. That's a little disappointing (it's supposed to be a malty beer), but as it does age, whatever is in there seems to get a bit more cohesive as a flavor composite. Pretty easy drinking, grain focused, seemingly getting more interesting with age.
