Brewed by jll23

Brewing Notes

Attempting to pitch this wort on top of the cake left over from my latest Landbier brew.

Mashing with about 1.5 gallons (made up of campden treated tap water and distilled water). Will "sparge" with about 1.75 gallons of just treated tap water.

I added a handful of rice hulls to the mash to aid with drainage. There's not a lot of rye in this recipe, but I'm just being careful.

Cooled the wort until 63F and then pitched it on the Kolsch yeast cake. It is unseasonably warm for February and the apartment has heated up to over 70F due to brewing/cooking. I am hopeful it will cool down to a more moderate temperature as fermentation begins.

Fermentation started quickly with so much yeast in the mix. I left for a few hours and when I returned, the air lock/stopper had blown off the carboy and a constant bubbling of yeast was being expelled onto the floor. Not ideal, and I really should have seen it coming. Blow off tube has been hooked up and the beer is currently bubbling away. Even with all the activity, it's not as though the beer itself ever got extremely hot. It's currently a degree or two over the ambient temperature of 65F. That's right around where I want it. Yeast character on this beer is a little less important than the first beer I fermented with it (and less controllable giving the pitch count). As long as it has some nice German character to it, I'll be happy. One thing I do wonder about is how long fermentation will last given how vigorous it's going and how quickly it got started.

Two days after brewing, fermentation slowed enough that I could swap out the tube for the airlock. The high point of fermentation seems to have been last night when the temperature reached 68-69. That's a bit higher than I was shooting for, but the weather has upended the status quo here. Should still be fine.

Ended up giving it about 10 days to primary and then threw it downstairs to cold crash for a couple of days before bringing it back up to settle for the remaining 2 days of the 2-week fermentation period.

Lost a lot of beer due to the massive yeast cake, but given the fact that the ingredients cost about $10 for this small batch I can't worry about it too much. That's the trade off. At least at bottling the flavors are excellent and I see no discernible flaws from reusing the cake (though maybe a little less in the way of esters given the larger yeast amounts).

Taste

At bottling, I am pretty pleased with the beer so far. The Munich and rye malts really go well together. Great bready flavors with that little bit of rye dryness/spiciness. The hops are less evident (I imagine most of the remaining hop matter from the later additions was expelled with the yeast eruption). There is some nice woody/spiciness from them, however. A hint of bitterness keeps things crisp enough despite all the sweet-ish malt flavoring from the Munich. Beer is still pretty cloudy.

After about two weeks, the beer seems very ready to drink. It is surprisingly bitter, though not unpleasantly so. Kind of like a bitter or pale ale level of clean bitterness, that goes well with the rye and Munich malts. As the bitterness has asserted itself, malts have taken on a bit more of a supporting role, but they're still quite evident. The rye remains great and the Munich, for as much as is in here, is not at all overpowering. A good amount of German hop aroma too. Pretty solid.

Really nice beer. Rye and Munich remain great together. Bitterness level is a very balanced one, keeping it from being super malty. Rye helps that too, with the sort of spicy tartness. Though the funky barnyard flavors are nowhere near as strong as in the second Landbier I made (with this very same yeast), there is a hint in there. Might be a little nicer in these smaller quantities. I think a lot of hop flavor has either blended in or faded by now, but this remains a really tasty beer. Would happily make it again, perhaps using another German ale yeast for comparison.

Just want to revisit this again, as it's been in the bottle for over a couple months. I am getting some lime zest aromas from the hops. It's mixed with some wine flavors from the yeast and then a kind of woody/pencil smell (which I think is related to the barnyard funk aromas). As it ages, it strikes me as sweeter than I initially perceived. It is definitely a malty beer.
