Mega Farm

an All Grain Saison by jll23

Brewed by jll23

On June 15, 2017

Brewing Notes

Mashing with about 2.25 gallons of water. Most of that is campden-treated tap water. ~.6 gal is distilled water. Mashing a little thick, but it's probably worth it on the rinsing end.

"Sparging" with about 1.5 gallons of campden-treated tap water.

Added some rice hulls to the mash towards the end, to aid with drainage.

The volume or total liquid in the carboy is comprised of the yeast/beer from the last farmhouse beer plus a little over 2.5 gallons from this beer. All in all, it's probably around 2.9 gallons, meaning there's not much head space. I'll have to keep a close eye on it, as a result.

Cooled the wort down to about 71F before pouring it on the yeast. Ambient temperature is around 74F.

Massive amount of yeast sediment claimed a lot of the beer, but that's life when pitching on the cake.

Taste

At bottling: My gravity sample was very yeasty, so most of my initial taste impressions are from the sweetened/primed remains that failed to fill the final bottle. Nice, pretty clear yellow-gold color. Smells quite pleasant. Nice yeast esters, reminiscent of Diminui. Seems to have a bit more spice/phenolics to it, which I actually think is a good thing. Balances the fruit and the spice pretty well. I'm not noticing much higher alcohol taste--seems pretty smooth--but at nearly 10% the beer is still making me sweat. The finish has a firmer bitterness to it, but I'm not tasting it that clearly right now with all the residual sugar. I think I'm getting a nice hint of round wheat flavor that goes really well with a beer of this type (and specifically the yeast). It's just slightly more interesting than a full pils farmhouse beer. I dare not read much more into this at this stage.

Trying this after 3 weeks in the bottle. This is actually the second time in a few days that I've cracked one. I can smell some booze, but the taste is pretty smooth and pleasant. The aroma is a combination of wheat malt and the somewhat subtle fruit from this particular yeast strain. Taste is fruity and definitely spicey, with some of that enhanced by the booze. I like it. It's not super intense, but a bit like a spicier tripel. I do smell the perle hops in there, contributing their particular fruitiness on top of the yeast. I definitely feel the strength after one of them.

Apparently I haven't documented this beer's progression very well. Tasting this after almost 5 months in the bottle. I think it's really nice and pleasant for a beer pushing 10%. The fruity esters made by this yeast are totally different from the French and Belgian saison yeasts I've used more often, but I think this one definitely has a place in the repertoire. I don't really want to make bold claims about commercial comparisons, but having just returned from a trip to Wisconsin, the yeast spice and malt flavors of Mega Farm remind me a little of Spotted Cow Grand Cru. Definitely doesn't have the actual spice flavors (coriander, to be exact) of that beer, but it's probably the wheat and the fruitiness of the yeast that invite comparison for me here. Oh, and the strength which invites a bit of spiciness of its own. Back to the beer at hand: smooth malt flavors, almost a little vanilla from the Belgian pils combined with wheat. Solid combination, put on a lot of booze and fruity yeast. I'm sure I've talked about Dingeman's pils before, but it seems totally different from German versions to me. So just maltier. It's not grainy at all, none of the corn elements. This is not to say I prefer it, moreso to voice my wonderment and how different the malt flavors are in these Belgian beers I've made with it. Perhaps some of that is what the yeasts are bringing to the equation and it would be interesting to try brewing a German style with Dingeman's to really see if I can still taste it. Anyway, this is a really nice, kind of straightforward farmhouse beer, with tons of booze. The malt flavors in conjunction with everything else, plus months of aging, are starting to remind me of a tripel. It's really like a farmhouse tripel, where the simple malts produce something that seems far more complex. I'd certainly consider this my most successful high gravity fermentation to date.
