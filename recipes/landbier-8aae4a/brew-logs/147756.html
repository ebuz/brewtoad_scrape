Brewing Notes

I went to a particular brewing store in order to get the white labs yeast, though they ended up being out. This time I am using Wyeast Kolsch 2565. I imagine this will still create a good result (I have used the Wyeast Germanic ale yeast varieties before), but I did really like the white labs one I used for the first brewing of this beer. Additionally, I'm not thrilled about the lack of clarity that the Wyeast versions are known for. We'll see.

I am planning to let this beer sit for two weeks before bottling. At that time, I will attempt to pitch another larger beer on the yeast cake (instant, productive starter).

For the mash, I used about half a gallon of distilled water mixed with one gallon of my tap water, with a bit of campden tablet mixed in. To sparge, I'm planning for about 2 gallons.

The hops I got this time around are 5.8% AA instead of the 5.3% I used the first time. I will compensate for this by simply adding the aroma/flavor hops later in the boil. That is the easiest thing I can do and it will get me within about an IBU of the original. It will be interesting to see how this move changes things.

Cooled the wort until it was 65F. Pitched most of the smack pack (but not all of it). Ambient temps right around 67F.

Decent mashing this time. Overshot expected OG by .004.

Taste

Bottling sample is still really cloudy, but gravity strongly suggests it's finished. The beer has a somewhat funky nose to it (which is in stark contrast to the last yeast I used to brew it), but the malt flavors are as great as I'd expect. It's got a nice light biscuity/bready flavor going on. I'm tempted to say the general flavor is a bit more in line with Kolsch than the white lab version I used previously. It's got that really light, dry, yeast flavor to it that just nudges the malt tastes in that direction, slightly.

I failed to wait long to try this--a little less than 2 weeks in the bottle. Have to say, I'm pretty impressed so far. Though it was only about 12 days ago (and after I initiated a second fermentation in the bottle) that the beer was super cloudy, it's quite brilliant now. The beer is about the same color as last time and the main malt flavors seem consistent (all basic, good news). Where it seems to differ significantly is in the aroma. I think this is what I forgot about German ales: they have their own fruity/resiny funk. Maybe the white labs yeast doesn't as much, and I still liked it a lot, but this is a whole new world of weirdly wonderful. The funk and the malt interplay in interesting ways, though it should be said that some of the shift might also be attributable to me moving back the aroma hops in the boil. Whatever the reason, the result gives off a kind of nuttiness that has more dimensions than the last time I brewed this. There are just a lot of weird/cool high notes that weren't there when using a "cleaner" yeast. It's really early, so I don't want to get too ahead of myself, but things are going in some intriguing directions here so far. Besides the change in late hop schedule, the beer is a little dryer this time, so I image that is changing things a little.

It's been a month now, so time to revisit how things turned out. This is a (second) physical manifestation of a fictionalized "state beer" of an actual place in Germany where my surname comes from (though I have never been to it). I'd like to think it tastes like that. The ingredients aren't far off from what you'd see in a Kolsch, but it's definitely not a Kolsch. It's really easy drinking, but a littler maltier than that, with a bit more hops than that, and maybe even more yeast aroma/flavor than you'd get in one of those. It still is really easy drinking. My partner referred to the taste as quite "mild" and if she's saying that, it's got to be pretty delicate. The hops have a woodsy/spicy German flavor to them with hints of fruits/light citrus. The malts come from Western Germany, the yeast, from Koln, and the hops are descendants of noble hops. The water is decidedly great lakes, with all the myriad pros and cons of that. It's like I said.

Just for posterity, it is worth noting that this is the funkiest German ale I have ever tasted. Barnyard level funk. Only really in the aroma--it has no real taste consequences. Interesting stuff, and not unpleasant. Worth noting in the event that one really wants to create a German version of what we typically think of as a Belgian farmhouse beer (this is probably the yeast to do it). Yeast didn't even get all that warm during fermentation. Anyway, all of this does make me wonder what German farmhouse beers looked like, back when that was the way they were made.
