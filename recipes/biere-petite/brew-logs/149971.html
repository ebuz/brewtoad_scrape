Brewed by jll23

Brewing Notes

Mashing with about 1.75 gallons. Most of that is campden treated tap water, but ~.5 gal is distilled water.

Will "sparge" with about 1.75 gallons of campden treated tap water.

Cooled the wort down to about 70F before pitching most of the smack pack (which at this point is a couple of months old). Said smack pack was sitting out over night, so as to get a jump on fermentation.

Mash outcome wasn't great, but it was as anticipated by the recipe. I can't imagine that having adding acidulated malt to an already copper color beer really did much for my pH situation, but I used it in spirit of the last beer I brewed with this yeast. I think a little acidity adds complexity, especially in "farmhouse" beers.

Ambient temperature is currently around 76F in here, but I expect it to drop significantly in the next few hours.

Wort had a lot of nice tastes going for it with the little amounts of specialty malts. Thought the bitterness was really nice. Understated, so far. We'll see once it dries out.

Taste

At bottling: Beer is a light amber color. Still kind of cloudy. Smells really malty, which is interesting because there's not all that much malty stuff in it. The beer is really surprisingly dry, but it doesn't necessarily taste that dry with the malt flavors (at least not now, uncarbonated). At first I didn't get a lot of hops, but now I'm definitely getting the black currant flavor. It's interesting and kind of lends to some tartness with the yeast finish. Obviously, a way stronger beer than I had intended, though I'm tasting a lot of that. From now on, I will just call this Biere, since it's not petite at all.

After two weeks, I finally got around to tasting this one. It's still sort of a light amber color. Lighter than an oktoberfest by a little. Two main things stand out to me. The hops are really interesting and the flavor is a significant part of the beer. It is a very mild, tart currant flavor, but there's also what strikes me as an orange flavor to it. It's that combination of acidity and fragrant aromatic citrus. The hop aromas are somewhat subtler than your typical flavor hops, but it's really interesting and potentially promising in conjunction with something else. They're pretty good here, and quite pristine on their own, I'm just thinking about greater flavor synergies. The other standout flavor is caravienne. I haven't used it since probably last summer and it's immediately identifiable. It's a malt that has kind of a spiciness and almost some subtle vanilla flavors. In my experience, it also takes a while for it to age and mellow. Right now, even the small amount in this beer is obvious and slightly less blended/refined than I might like. I expect that aspect will be good in time.

A few days later (less than a week). When is the last time you had a orange dream-sickle beer? Well this is that beer. It's all about the caravienne and the hops. As a blend it's something totally unique and awesome. This beer is a little bit lighter than Mai im Sumpf. Dark orange, not quite red. Slightly malty, with vanilla flavors. Aromas of citrus and those distinctively acidic currants. Truly new and seemingly awesome.

After about a month in bottle, malt aroma is very reminiscent of honey. Aroma is still very much focused on currants and other fruit. Yeast is still gloriously soft and balanced. Seems a lot sweeter than it is. A family member who tried it described the beer as smelling like a fruit cocktail cup. I don't know if that's great, but I kind of see how she could get it. That aside, it's a pretty nice beer, and the flavors are fairly refined, even if they are quite unique.

Circling back after trying Suds for the first time since bottling (as a comparison). I have to say, the caravienne in this beer is so key. That's the contrast in this one's favor. It's the malt that makes this taste so creamy and gives the more vanilla notes. And it's fantastic. That, the glorious yeast that somehow elevates the beer far beyond it's FG, and the criminally underrated Brewer's Gold hops, circa 1934. If Suds is a pale ale or IPA version of this weird yeast and hop combo, then this is something considerably more complex and interesting, due to the malt. This is an easy win. I simplified my last biere de garde grain bill (based on all the malty brewing I've done in the last year) and mixed in these weird hops (which were designated on the package as good for the style, whatever that means). I am somewhat stunned as to how solid of a combination this is. It may not be the exact style I was shooting for, but it is a clear style combination nonetheless. Few and far between are the times you realize you created something unique and cohesive (probably by accident). For me, this is one of those times.

We are 2+ months in the bottle at tasting. The fascinating hop flavors are on the decline. So are the unique malt flavors of the caravienne. Hop flavors decline faster than malts, but this is still an amazing combination. That is the story of the beer. I also, just tasted Suds before this, like the last time (weird way to taste beer, but I apparently have some motivating factor for repeating this). That beer has a lot less malt, more booze spice, and as I recently realized, way more funk. There might be some in here, but it's not very noticeable (and I've detected it in German ale yeasts before). Mostly it smells of weird (80+ year old hop varieties), tastes of glorious caravienne malt, and then sits on a slightly Belgiany, but mostly straightforward and pleasant, Biere de Garde yeast.
