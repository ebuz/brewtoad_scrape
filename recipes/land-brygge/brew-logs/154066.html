Brewed by jll23

On May 05, 2018

Brewing Notes

Mashing with about 1.5 gallons. ~.5 is distilled water, the rest is tap water treated with a little campden.

Will "sparge" with a little over 1.75 gallons of campden-treated tap water.

The bittering charge requires a lot of hops at this weak level of AA%. I decided to add a bit of them before the boil, kind of trying out first wort hopping. Might net me a little more IBU's, we'll see.

Tossed in a partial pellet during the start of cooling. Can't seem to help myself.

Chilled wort to around 70F and pitched the entirety of a yeast pack that was a little less than 2 months old. Put it in a closet and will forget about it for a bit.

Unlike the saison yeast I used recently, it appears that this yeast's attenuation can be manipulated with mash temperature. It finished higher than I imagined it would, but that's good to know for the future. This strain seems pretty flexible in terms of what it could be good in.

Taste

Wort tastes pretty sweet, but there is noticeable bitterness at the end. Seems like it will balance out well (as was the aim) assuming the beer doesn't finish too low. The beer appears like it will be a darker golden color.

At bottling, the malt taste on this one is really good. I forgot how much I like the Schill malt. Also, this yeast has some interesting notes to it, but they're pretty subtle. Makes me believe I have a shot at making things similar to German ales with it. Just a little bitterness at the end. Quite nice so far.

Tried one of these a little over a week after bottling. It's kind of a copper color. What stood out first was how uncarbonated it was at this point. I used probably too much priming sugar and most of it is just sitting in the beer. That seems a little odd, since the ambient temperature has been in the 70's for most of the week. Anyway, hopefully that will sort itself out, but it's weighing heavily on taste so far. Things tasted quite sweet, obviously. The most striking flavors were seemingly from the yeast in the form of honey and fruit aromas. I didn't expect them to be so noticeable. In fact, I was told the yeast was pretty "clean" in its profile. I guess it's not super estery, but those flavors loom heavily over the background. They're like additional hop/malt flavors. I imagine things will eventually blend with age, but at least right now, with all the residual sugars and lack of carbonation the sweet yeast flavors are really in the forefront. I guess I'd also say that if these flavors persist one should be careful not to use hops with sweet fruit flavors or malts that lean towards the honey end of the spectrum. I imagine they could easily just become too reinforced with this yeast. That's my first impression, at least.

At a little over two weeks in the bottle, it's finally got decent carbonation. Dried out considerably and tastes a lot better. Definitely in the realm of a German ale taste. The grain aromas are great again, and the noble hop flavor is just enough to add an interesting layer to what is otherwise a malt-focused beer. Beyond all of that there are the interesting, esters that seem to come out of nowhere. Fruity, what I think of as orange, and then some sweet honey smells. All of that goes much better with the other aspects, now that it's less sweet. The beer is still somewhat cloudy, and it may not get much clearer. Kind of an orange color. Definitely a nice easy drinking beer. Not bitter, but just bitter enough to keep it from being too sweet. More or less as I had hoped it would be.

Few days under a month in bottle. Carbonation has come up in this one, but the bubbles are large and not well incorporated into the beer. The head retention is pretty poor, as a result. It has a really nice grain flavor to it, but there's also considerable noble hop flavor which is eventually superseded by the crazy yeast aromas. I'm drinking it out of a different glass this time (stemless wine glass as opposed to Belgian tulip). I get the honey still, but the fruit is finally, definitively like mango. When I say that, I mostly mean there're aromas in there as if I had used Amarillo. Truly interesting stuff. What I will say is that perhaps there's slightly too much residual sugar. The honey aromas just blow it out of proportion. This beer does have a very thick texture to it, no doubt coming from the same place. It's interesting, but it might be a little more balanced if things were slightly more crisp/dry.

Still a shade under a month in the bottle. Had my first gusher of the new beers here. There was so much foam initially, but it dissipated pretty quickly. A nice stir of the glass will summon it again, but in general there's not a lot of head retention with this beer. I'm not sure how to register that, exactly. There was a little cara malt in this recipe, but not a lot. In general, I will say that the I haven't seen a lot of head retention from this yeast, but a lot of that could be recipe dependent. I'm getting a lot of spalt character mixed into this beer. It's awesome and, perhaps most interestingly, the aroma reminds me so much of German Spalt Select hops, my go-to noble-ish hops for quite sometime, under whose guidance I cut my teeth on German ales. It's just a touch less fruity in terms of lime or citrus, but still quite nobly mild and pleasant. Great counterweight to sweet malt aromas/tastes. It forms its own context. I will say that the bubbles on this one are a smaller than they have been, which I hope portends better incorporation. The scent of this beer is fantastic, despite the rough start to this pour. Great noble hop aroma plus fantastic German malts, and a bit of weird fruitiness from the Nordic yeast. Speaking of which, it does do a pretty decent job of subbing in for German ale yeast, just fruitier in a different way. Definitely not the same, but surprisingly okay and perhaps good in its own right.

About 6 weeks in the bottle. The carbonation finally seems to have integrated itself into the beer. The bubbles seem a lot smaller and don't all completely dissipate within a minute of pouring. The aroma of this beer is really nice. It's come together a lot more than I thought it would. It has an intensely delicious bready malt aroma, a little bit of the kveik fruit aromas, and then the really excellent spalt hop fragrances. Things have dried out a lot, but it's still slightly on the sweet/malty side. Hop bitterness is noticeable so it keeps things pretty close to balanced. Kind of an orangey/copper color, with some haze. I'm beginning to like this one quite a bit. The flavor combos really are pretty solid. All the Germanic ingredients really go a long way to making it seem like a German ale, even if the Norwegian notes remain.
