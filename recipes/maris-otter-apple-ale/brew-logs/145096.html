Maris Otter Apple Ale

an All Grain Fruit Beer by jll23

Brewed by jll23

Brewing Notes

Fermentation stats for this currently do not include the cider portion of this beverage. The OG is about 1.061 as 2 gallons of beer. I cooled the beer to about 68 before pitching. Utilizing the ol' wet towel and fan method to help keep the temperature from rising too far too fast. I will give the beer 2-3 days to ferment on its own before adding the cider, which I have yet to acquire.

Even though I used filtered water this time for the mash, it made no appreciable difference on my efficiency. Ultimately it's the same tap water, just run through a britta filter. Efficiency continues to suck, in the low-mid 60 percents.

I've added minimal priming sugar this time around, keeping with the quasi-British theme. Additionally, this allows me to hedge against the possibility of wild yeast present in the unpasteurized cider fermenting out the remaining long chain sugars.

Taste

At bottling, the beer is a bit fruity, a bit malty, a little tart, a little funky, with a bit of bitterness at the end. I would be hard pressed to say that any of these flavors have yet to come together, but 3+ weeks of total aging time is pretty low for cider. I don't love the flavors now (at least not as parts of a whole), but I'll give it some time and see what happens. The fact that I taste bitterness is a little perplexing since when I take into account the 1 gallon dilution from the cider, we should be looking at something like 15 IBU's. That is just enough to curtail bacteria, but not very much for a beer in the 6%+ range. If things don't improve, I can at the very least blend some of this with a really sweet munich beer I made previously.

At about a month in the bottle, I still don't love the outcome. A lot of that is probably my fault though. I made a string of very carbonated beers prior to this one and since it had a lot of British ingredients, I decided to err on the side of very little carbonation. Also, I was somewhat concerned that the unpasteurized cider would carry with it wild yeast that might eventually eat through any remaining long sugars from the malt portion. The drink has some decent apple flavor, but there's also a rough apple bitterness coming through that additional carbonation would have helped balance. I say this because, by luck, I tried one bottle that seemingly received more than its fair share of the priming sugar and was quite effervescent as a result. This particular one had none of that sharpness to it, though in return the apple taste was also more muted. It tasted surprisingly like the cherry beer I made a few months ago, an interesting twist considering that beer was comprised of Belgian beer ingredients and this one is, again, quite British in origin. All that is to say, it tasted like a dry, acidic fruit beer, where the fruit itself was more of an accent note than the main flavoring. There's still some chance that the carbonation will increase as small amounts of wild yeast work on the remaining sugars, but it's unlikely that there will be anything more than mild carbonation in this beer. Additionally, the cider portion could still mellow out--it's only been a month.

On 11/15/2016, I sampled another bottle, this one clearly with some wild yeast floating around the neck. It seems that for some of these at least, my fears of brett re-fermentation were founded. Frankly, that seems pretty okay at this point. This one has more reasonable carbonation and a really sweet fruity smell. There is some grainy flavor from the malts, but I also get some funk. I think the beer has got a ways of aging to go, but it's pretty interesting if this is the direction it's going in. The only thing I might have changed about this had I known it would get funky is perhaps using a Belgian yeast to better balance with those flavors. This British/US yeast isn't bad with it, but I just think something of a farmhouse nature might have been ideal.

I'm tasting what I believe is the final bottle almost exactly a year after bottling. Pretty carbonated, so my worries about that initially were misplaced (shocking). I've always liked the aroma of this beer and that remains true. It has subtle, not particularly funky, wild yeast notes mixed with apple-y flavors. The aroma is still stronger than the actual taste. Some mild cidery flavors, but still some sharpness from the apples too. It's a vaguely fruity beer, but all in all nothing very strong in terms of flavors. Pleasant enough beer, but between the residual sharpness and the lack of stand out positive flavors, this a kind of middling result. Still seems like a solid idea. I remain convinced that a more interesting Belgian yeast might have better complimented what's already here. There are already some interesting esters from the apple.
