Biscuit Primer

an All Grain Belgian Specialty Ale by jll23

Brewed by jll23

On January 01, 2018

Brewing Notes

Pilsner malt is from Avangard. Biscuit is Dingeman's.

Mashing with about 1.5 gallons. ~.25 of that is distilled water, the rest is campden-treated tap water.

Will sparge with a little less than 2 gallons of campden-treated tap water.

Cooled the wort down to ~66F before pitching the entire smack pack. I'm not opposed to fermenting this one on the cool side.

The wort is bready and just on this side of bitter.

Taste

At bottling, the sample has a great biscuity aroma and taste. It is a lovely golden color, and quite clear. Drinks surprisingly sweet.

Trying one about two weeks after bottling. Gentle carbonation. Drinks a little dryer with carbonation, which is welcome. The biscuit is still really nice, but a little more blended into the beer now. I taste more of the hops now. Nice herbal spice mixed with a little citrus fruit. Still has a hint of yeastiness to it, but I don't mind. There's a little spice from the Belgian fermentation byproducts. A combination of light fruit and clove spice. This one is a mild quaff, but pretty damn tasty and interesting. Never had one quite like this, in terms of flavor combinations. That's what I was going for.

About 3 weeks post bottling. Easy drinking beer, but quite unique. One noteworthy thing is that there's a lot more banana ester in this one than when I ferment with this yeast at higher temps (or larger gravities). That said, it's still way milder than a weizen yeast. I smell the fruit esters and the mild spice, along with the bready notes. The biscuit has gotten a lot milder, but it's still one of the noticeable hallmarks of the beer. The finish is where the malt really shines. It changes what would normally just be a fruity/spicy Belgian beer into something way more complex. I think it'd go really well with wheat, just to ramp up the bread flavors and make them multi-dimensional. I couple years ago I made a series of hoppy Belgian beers that were meant to be big on amarillo flavor. I never quite got it right, and always felt like something was missing. It could be this final biscuit flavor. It goes damn well with Belgian yeast and a bit of hops.

We're about 6-8 weeks in bottle and I've had most of the batch, but haven't noted flavor findings for quite a while. This is a really easy drinking Belgian-y beer, but the subtleties are amazing. It's not a bitter beer, with the bready flavors it drinks somewhat sweet, but you wouldn't really know it with the biscuit balance and the yeast. That's what this beer is about. I spoke previously about the banana yeast, and that remains a really interesting aspect of this beer, but there's also a slightly clove-y spice that when combined is a whole new take on the traditional hefeweizen ester/phenol marriage. The biscuit malt only tweaks the spice reading on this beer. The aroma starts with the banana/fruity notes and ends with the clove and bread spice. I don't know if the combination would be wildly popular, but it is more than worthy of being its own regional specialty. It remains very easy drinking and could be the basis of a far wider hit. Most of this success (and when I drink it I think it was wildly successful) was perhaps the lower fermentation temperature. It's one of those beers that is more about a few fundamentals executed well than anything else, but damn are those aspects done well. Glorious beer.

Notes

Date Note
01/02/2018 3:51pm

Fermentation began sometime last night and is proceeding quite slowly. I have never had this specific problem before, but with the frequent, external sub-zero temperatures of the last few days, the temperature of the carboy is currently too low. It's somewhere around 63F, and for the first time ever, I am struggling to heat it up. The thermostat is set for 70 right now, but that doesn't seem to be translating to the ground where the carboy is, even though it's less than 10 feet away. I wrapped it in towels a few hours ago, hoping that I could capture the internal heat of the yeast, but so far that hasn't made much difference. Next, I'm going to try using a heating pad to warm up a blanket and then wrap the carboy in it. The funny thing about all this is that if I just made a German ale, these conditions would be fine. I just figured it was going to be hard to keep the temperature this low with the heater running all the time and therefore went the "safe" route with a Belgian beer. Lesson learned.

01/02/2018 8:00pm

I've been using an electric heating pad on the lowest setting and a blanket to gently boost the fermentation temperature. Currently, we're sitting around 66F, which is at least within the acceptable range. Fermentation is progressing in a relatively constant manner, but it's not terribly vigorous yet.

01/02/2018 10:16pm

Heating pad is still in use, and the temperature has risen to about 68F. That's more in line with what I initially envisioned. I only pitched the yeast a little over 24 hours ago, so the timeline really wasn't all that delayed.

01/03/2018 12:09am

Fermentation temperature got up to about 70, so I turned the heating pad off.

01/03/2018 3:40am

Based on the airlock activity, I'd say we're finally at the vigorous stage of fermentation. The temperature is flirting with a climb above 70F, so I'm removing some layers around the fermentor and hope that this will subtly discourage further increase. I'm about ready to be done messing with this one, but I also don't want to see the temperature crash rapidly.
01/03/2018 3:39pm

At the height of vigorous fermentation the temperature might have briefly been around 72F, but it has since slowed and cooled back to 70F.
