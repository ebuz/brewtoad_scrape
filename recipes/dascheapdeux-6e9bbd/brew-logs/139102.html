DasCheapDeux

an All Grain Russian Imperial Stout by Oliver Harris

  • 1 Brew Logs
  • 0 Variants

Print
Export BeerXML

Brewed by Oliver Harris

On November 09, 2015

Brewing Notes

Mash: Mashed in at 163 with no flame and covered, eventually temp got to 152 and i reheated the kettle but forgot about the heating and came back to a 165 temp! Turned flame off and left the lid slightly off to allow some heat to escape and extended the mash time to 75min, but it barely cooled down past 162F.

Sparge: I didn't use sparge water, but I also learned that I don't have a colander large enough to fit into the kettle and keep the grains not submerged in the pre-wort. Also, in the double-mesh strainer i used as a colander, the width of the bag was too large for the strainer so some runnings from the grains poured over the side of the kettle (approx .125gal). After waiting about 10min for grains to drain, I brought them inside and put them in a colander over a 5gal bottling bucket to collect more runnings while the pre-wort got to a boil. Collected about .5 gal of runnings and added back to pre-wort right before boil.

Boil: I noticed that I was over my targeted boil volume, so I increased the flame and left the lid slightly off (using the mash paddle as a wedge) hoping evaporation would help me reach my targeted post-boil volume. Hop additions went as planned for 75min. Added wort chiller to the kettle at the 15min mark to sanitize it (did not pump cold water).

Cool: I had an ice bath with a pond pump just waiting to accept as much heat from the wort as possible. Between brewdays I take old seltzer bottles (.375L), fill with water, and freeze them. I had about 6 at the start with about 3 gal normal cold water flowing around them. I was able to crash from 212F to 160F within minutes, and then from 160F to 100F took a bit longer (~15min). From 100F to 60F was the longest, most arduous part of the cooling effort. Once the ice from the seltzer bottles melted about halfway, I would take the chiller output (warm water) and shove the tubing down into the seltzer bottle to melt the ice. Once all the ice in a given bottle was melted, I would place the empty bottle next to me and start melting another bottle's ice chunk. After 3 were empty, I grabbed 3 more bottles from the freezer and repeated the process. All in all I think I used about 13 seltzer bottles to get down to 60F and it took a long time.

Xfer: This has been an issue with my new kettle. The bayou kettle came with a thermometer, spigot, mesh screen to attach to the spigot inlet (looks like a mesh hose sort of), and a false bottom. My first two brews using this kettle I had a lot of trouble getting the entire volume to flow through the spigot into the bucket/carboy. In those brews I was using pellet hops, and I learned that the rate limiting phenomena was that hop particulate would coagulate and block the mesh screen to the spigot, thus preventing flow out. In this brew, I used only leaf hops in hopes of improving the Xfer time.

At first, the flow out of the spigot was fast and steady and I thought I had finally figured out the issue. However, eventually the spigot ran near to dry, and none of the outlet flow was entering my bucket and splashing on the floor. I decided to scoop up hop leaves from the top of the false bottom and place them in a bag, which helped the flow, albeit the success was short lived. Eventually the coagulate formed again and blocked the wort flow through the mesh screen and thus out the spigot. I then scooped all of the leaves off the false bottom, removed the FB, and began moving all of the hop leaves i could collect (from the botom of the kettle and along the mesh screen) to the opposite side of the spigot. This worked for the most part, but I had to continually scrape the area near the spigot and the mesh screen to free the flow from obstruction.

All in all, this aspect of the brewday was inefficient and wasteful, as about .25 gal of wort ended up on the floor.

Pitch: So since this recipe was aiming for cost efficiency, I used some dry yeast as opposed to my go-to liquid yeast. Dry yeast adds a few additional steps in the process, which can get a bit overkill when you are having a hectic brewday (like today). Once I hit the 30min mark on my boil, I rehydrated the yeast in 95F water (not pre boiled, OOF!) and stirred with a (slightly, OOF!) sanitized brewing thermometer to let the yeast wake up. They woke up and were acting like good little yeasties do, so I proceeded to pitch them to the ~5gal wort.

Brix: Initial brix measurement of 25.2, corresponding to 1.1066 OG. This is higher than expected, and I think that has to do with either (1) higher mash temp; or (2) longer mash time (75min).

Cleanup: UGH. Took forever just because in the heat of the moment I ended up using so many buckets, thermos, etc. and I spilled so much wort on the floor.

Time: Started at 4pm finsihed cleanup around 10-10:30pm. This time included eating dinner and taking Trigo on a minor walk.

+++++++_ Xfer to 2ndary: 11/21/15; xfered to 2ndary carboy, added bourbon soaked oak cubes and 1oz centennial leaf hops. No brix measurement. Washed yeast for use next time.

Xfer to 3rdary: 11/30/15; removed oak cubes and hop leaves and transfered to carboy for final ferm period. Brix 11%, 1.0442
