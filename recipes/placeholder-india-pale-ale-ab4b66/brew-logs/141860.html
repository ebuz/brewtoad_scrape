Brewing Notes

Brew day!

Going to head down to the brewshop in a bit to start gathering ingredients. Estimated costs:

7lbs Pale Ale = $12.60 5.5lbs 2-row = $9.90 12oz Carapils = $1.70 10oz Munich Light = $1.40 8oz Caramel 35L = $1.00 Grain = $26.60

2oz Columbus (using 1.5) 2oz Cascade (using 1.5) 2oz Chinook (using 2) 3oz Centennial (using 2.5) 1oz Citra (using 1) 1oz Simcoe (using 1) 11 hops = $30.80

Yeast = $7.50

Total = $64.90

Substitutions: No Pale Ale malt, so just struck that and increased use of 2-row to 12.5 lbs to account for it. No WLP 060, so used California WLP001 (Chico) instead. No Simcoe at shop so we'll need to get that.

Brewtime! ———————————————————

2:47pm — Started 4.6 gallons of water. Will raise water temp to 166°F before adding grain. Mash temp should be 153°F for 60 minutes.

3:24pm — started the mash about 5 minutes ago. Got it to a temp of 153°F, maybe a little over, but it will be fine. We'll begin heating up the sparge water in a minute.

4:50pm — we just finished the sparge. The mash was fine, and then we sparged roughly 2.4 gal of water at 168°F over the grains while sitting on an oven rack. Added FWH of 0.5 oz Columbus. Now we wait for the boil to start!

7:35pm — Boil began and ended. Hops all added according to schedule (had to modify because I added one centennial at 20 mins instead of 10). We just finished the beer, cooled it down using wort chiller and ice, and filled up the carboy. We generated just 3 gal of beer instead of a more expected 4 or so.

9:44pm — the beer's been sitting for a while. We've realized that we have some learnings from this and should have scaled the beer differently. We aimed for a 5.5 batch size, meaning that's the combined sparge runnings we started the boil with. That netted us just 3 gallons of finished beer, and now that that's settled, at least half a gallon of that isn't beer at all. Leaving us with 2.5 gallons of beer, or 320 oz, or just 14 22oz beers. We have so much more room in the carboy and in the kettle, we could have easily doubled this recipe. More sensible, maybe, to scale it appropriately up to 8 gallons.

What we need to realize is that if we have 5.5 gallons of runnings (if we even had that) we only netted 2.5 gallons of beer in the carboy. Which means, if we went for 11 gallons of combined runnings (into the boil), we would net 5 gallons of final beer.

That would mean doubling our entire recipe, which would mean 29 lbs of grain in the beer! And 20oz of hops! To keep the same efficiency. What we could also do is cut the efficiency a bit — so shoot for 11 gallons of combined runnings but nerf the beer. Say, scale the recipe 150% but the liquids 200%. Going to do a quick hypothetical test of this in Brewtoad. Signing off, 10:02pm.

———

Bottling time!

We have 2.5 gallons of beer (1 gallon of beer = roughly 8.345lbs; carboy weighed 35lbs, empty 5-gallon carboy weighs 13.5lbs, so 21.5lbs of beer = 2.5 gallons). That means we have 320 oz of beer and need 14 or so 22oz bottles to take it in.

The bottles have sanitized now.

We are ready to bottle! We need to heat up the water and sugar to add to the beer. 2.5 gallons mean 2.5oz of sugar in 1/2 cup of water.

We got 11 bottles.
