Brewed by jll23

On May 31, 2017

Brewing Notes

Mashing with about 1.7 gallons of water. Most of that is campden treated tap water, but .5 gallon of it is distilled water.

Will sparge with about 1.8 gallons of treated water.

Swapped in Santiam hops at the last minute. Brew store didn't have my go to pseudo-noble variety. I did try a commercial beer recently with this hop featured in it and thought it had excellent German hop funk/spice, so I'm excited to see how it turns out.

Managed to overshoot OG while also putting what looks like a lot into the carboy. Here's hoping the tube won't be necessarily, though the smart ones plan for such contingencies.

Bought a new funnel for the first time in years. Stupidly thought it would be cool to get a screen with it. Used said screen, which quickly became overrun with hop matter, rendering it useless. Soldiered on, removing the screen as soon as I could. Things proceeded as usual, thereafter.

Cooled the wort to about 75F before pitching most of the smack pack. At this point it is about two months old. I prefer to underpitch for these sorts of things, but time was not exactly on my side. Going to try to ferment in the mid 70's, but the weather seems like it might be both over and under that in the coming weeks.

Wort tastes of light malt with a good amount of bitterness.

Taste

At bottling, it's a nice light gold color. It has some solid saison aroma (fruit and spice). It definitely has some bitterness to it, at the end. We'll see how it ages and melds with a fair amount of carbonation.

After two weeks in the bottle. Very yellow-gold in color. I guess I will say that while the yeast aroma is pleasant, it is somewhat restrained. Perhaps I should have pitched less. It has some nice fruity flavors, but it's not all that phenolic. Decent level of carbonation at this time. Pretty big bubbles and decent head retention for a beer mashed low. This is the second time in the last few months that I've made a Belgian beer comprised all or mostly of Dingeman's pils. I am really surprised how full and malty the beer seems using that malt. It's quite impressive, but I also sometimes feel that it fights against the dryness one expects from a beer like this with a very low FG. This beer is nice and pleasant as it is, but I'd like it to be a little more dry as time goes on. I also have to reconsider bitterness a bit if I'm going to continue using this base malt (which is to say it can handle more). I will say the hop flavor this time does go pretty well with the esters, even if the whole thing seems sweeter than I think it should. Pretty damn clear for a saison.

Three weeks in the bottle. Still insanely clear and light yellow. I continue to swear that this pils malt is far more full-bodied than any other kind I remember using. Nice ester content, but some of them are a little more refined than others. It's a matter of the same old slightly unruly funk aromas. In general this is better than previous ones, I just wonder if it's an aging issue or something else. Seems like a cleanly made beer, all in all. There's just a little saison yeast funk in there that I sometimes like more than others. This is nowhere close to my first rodeo, or my 5th. I know the process well and I still occasionally puzzle at some of the more complex higher aromas in these farmhouse beers. Generally, I am quite positive about this one. It has a pretty nice fruity scent. The hops seem good. That is to say, both in terms of the pleasant fruitiness they contribute to the aroma and the bitterness, which is not too extreme, but just good enough for such a dry beer. Not as bitter as a commercial version, but close enough to my taste. I like the yeast character enough on its own that I don't need a distinct bitter punch to the face with each sip.

The beer has been in the bottle for two months. Upon tasting it for the first time in a while, I am struck by both how aged it seems and how sweet/non-bitter it drinks. There is a good deal of saison fruit, but not much funk or spice. This is reinforced by the Dingemans pils, which I maintain is one of the stronger/sweeter tasting pils malts. It's not a bad beer, but with freshness fading, it's clear that a stronger bitter edge would have gone a long way to make this thing drink more like a saison. As it is, it tastes more like a blonde or some such mild thing with fruity notes. Yeah, it is a dry beer, but even comprised of solely pils malt it seems very sweet/fruity. Not necessarily negative in its own right, but having recently tried some of the other beers I've made in the "farmhouse" spectrum, it needs a bit more bite or, dare I say, temperament. The pils tastes a little syrupy in conjunction with the esters, and some of that is age, but it makes what was a conservatively bittered saison taste downright toothless. That's not right. Certainly not a bad beer, and it's the first I made in about a year. Still, always room for improvement. This is a random, last throw away comment, but with the malt flavor in some ways this tastes like Belgian beer made through a German lens of maltiness. There's probably some personal description in there, but it's not inaccurate.

We're a little over 6 months in the bottle, tasting what I think is the final example of this beer. I've been keeping this one in the fridge for at least a month and it's made the beer surprisingly clear. Damn near pilsner clear. The Belgiany, yeast-based flavors of this beer (which were the focus) are mostly faded, but they persist. There's a little funk in there yet. The Belgian base grain is still pretty sweet (for a pils), as has been my experience, but it's a little more muted after all this time. Good, high carbonation, without gushing. Keeps some solid white head. A little bit of spice from the yeast, though not much with this strain. Kind of mineral-y. Seems like a decent saison. I enjoyed the difference/novelty in this yeast (though I have used it before), and while it has a nice territory all its own, it shrinks a bit (especially over time) when compared to the classic/dominant strain. As one might expect, the balance is different.
