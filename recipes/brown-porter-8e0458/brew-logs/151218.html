Brewing Notes

Mashing with a little under 1.5 gallons. ~.33 of that is distilled water, the rest is campden-treated tap water.

Will "sparge" with about 2 gallons of campden-treated tap water.

Going to mash lower this time, based on the last beer I made with this yeast. There's so much malt in here, a little dryness is probably good.

I am a little worried about all the dark malt, but we'll see what happens. Mash smells very roasty and chocolatey. Kind of like coffee. Wort tastes a little roasty, but not super aggressive. Wonder how fermentation will alter things.

Cooled the wort down to about 72F before pitching it on the yeast cake from my brown ale.

Taste

I was really worried about this beer being way too roasty, but based on the gravity sample I'm drinking (from bottling), it's a really pleasant beer. Sure, there is a ton of roast aroma and flavor (it smells almost like coffee). There's also more sweetness and more chocolate notes than I feared there would be. These things help balance the roast. In general it's not a very bitter beer, but there is a touch of astringency at the end. Not enough to spoil it though. Roasty beers should probably have a hint of that on the finish anyway. The yeast flavors are really nice in combination with the malt flavors (in addition to the chocolate and the roast, there are some nutty bread flavors). I think I tend to prefer this yeast when it's on the cleaner side (and this being the second time I was using the cake, it definitely had the numbers to keep esters subdued). It's a really clear, dark brown color. Looking forward to seeing how it is with some carbonation. So far, it's a hearty fall beer.

I've been sneaking bottles of this beer after a little less than two weeks post bottling. It's a little roasty, but not even close to how intense I thought it would going to be. It's barely there, towards the end. Otherwise, it's a lot of dark malt, a bit nutty, with chocolate, and even some interesting raisin-esque notes. Really complex on the malt side, obviously. The yeast is nice and creamy. Kind of subtle. Hop bitterness is in there somewhere, but other than it working with all the dark/roasted malt to make the beer taste a little dry (and balance out some of the sweeter malt flavors), it's way in the background. Color is dark brown, just barely translucent. Ultimately, I really like how this turned out. It's low alcohol, but you wouldn't know it. Tastes like a full, satisfying beer.

About a month after bottling, the beer is less sweet and more carbonated (naturally). The glorious malty flavors are a little dampened, but still the key for the beer. It's dark brown, smells like sweet malt, chocolate, roast, and raisin with some anise notes. The British yeast goes really well with all those flavors, and adds a creaminess. It's possible that even less carbonation is called for in this beer. It's not bad as is, but as is often the case, it sort of dries out and bubbles up as it ages in that first month. It's best as a luxurious malt experience, and perhaps that favors smooth sweetness over carbonation. Still smells and tastes great, with flavors that I have never before experienced in a porter or stout. Those are the revelatory stars. Still really good, just has more of a carbonic bite.

Finished off the last bottle of this about 4.5 months post bottling. Very carbonated, but the taste was excellent. All the great malt flavors remained. There's the little bit of roast paired with what I think is raisin-y and perhaps slightly anise-y. The flavor combinations are very unique, at least where I come from, and together they create an awesome recipe. Great, full-flavored small beer that I wouldn't think was small. Would make this one again in a heartbeat.
