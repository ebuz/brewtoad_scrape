Citrus Strong Ale

an All Grain Belgian Golden Strong Ale by jll23

Brewed by jll23

On August 15, 2018

Brewing Notes

Will mash with a little under 2 gallons of water. About .5 gallons of that is distilled and the rest is campden-treated tap water.

Will "sparge" with about 1.5 gallons total. About .25 of that is distilled with the remainder again being campden-treated tap water.

Going to try to mash pretty low with this one. It's a reasonably large beer, and while the honey may continue to ferment slowly over time, I don't want the FG to be too high when it's time to bottle.

I first-worted a few of the hops from the bittering charge, mostly because this is a thing I like doing currently.

I eye-balled the honey measurement, so there might be a bit more than half a pound in there. As long as it's under 10%, I think it won't be overpowering. We'll see. I should also note that the honey was added around the 15 minute remaining mark of the boil.

Today's boil seemed really sluggish. I used the same exact burner that I always do, but the energy level just wasn't there. I may end up with a bit more wort than intended as a result.

Tossed a pellet or two in at the start of chilling, just for more citrus flavor.

Higher than anticipated OG (though not super far off) probably due to adding more honey.

Cooled the wort to about 80F, loosely draped a blanket over it, and put it in the back of a closet. This is the strategy that seems to be working for me with these yeasts. Hopefully I didn't screw up a good thing with a bunch of hops and honey.

Taste

Wort sample is probably a golden color. Most of the color would have come from the honey, kettle caramelization, or the honey itself caramelizing. I can smell the honey in the sample. It tastes sweet (there's a lot of natural sugars in this wort), but there's also a surprising amount of bitterness in the background. I really hope the honey flavors aren't too strong. I hate to mess up a beer that creates so many of its own aromatic esters.

The bottling sample is a slightly cloudy light gold. The aroma of this one is definitely sweet citrus. First flavor impressions are also citrus. Both orange and lemon. It has a fairly viscous texture to it with a slightly sweet impression. The honey seems pretty subtle at first. The more I drink of it the more warming I get as it goes down my throat. There is some bitterness at the end that seems to build up over time. That could even be from the honey. Pretty interesting. It got a lot drier than projected, so I'm hoping that means most of the honey has been fermented out. Tried to go low on the priming sugar just in case there are sugars yet to be digested.

I realize now that I haven't really reviewed this and it's been quite a while in the bottle. So, there were some significant sub-optimal elements to this brew from the beginning, most notably that the honey seemed to add a pretty significant pseudo-bitterness to the otherwise mild beer. That never sat particularly well with what is a pretty strong beer that has its own slight booze notes. The good news is I think age has mellowed some of those rough edges. The bad news is, of course, that the fruit/citrus flavors that the concept was built around also fade with time. Honestly, over the past month and a half I haven't really been dying for many strong beers. That said, when I crack this open now it's pretty pleasant. It smells sweet between the hop/honey/yeast notes and that really goes a long way to balancing out any harsher spots in the profile that remain. There is still a lot of citrus in there, and it's a joint effort by the yeast and the hops. A pretty good beer for what was a complex reach of a recipe. Furthermore, I think it proves the concept pretty well. Maybe honey isn't the way to go, or maybe I just need to find way better honey for a project like this. Either way, the hops and yeast are on board, it's just a matter of fine-tuning hop additions/quantities/varieties to really compliment the strong ale base. It's nice, it'll mess you up easily, and it actually seems to be getting better with age.

Notes

Date Note
08/16/2018 3:46am

Fermentation has begun. It is somewhat slow a this point, but the airlock is gradually bubbling. Temp seems above 80F. There isn't a lot of head space on this one, part of the reason I had to discard perfectly good wort. However, I don't think the magical elixir will push the envelope in terms of what's left. There's a bit of gas room, and we'll probably fill it with eventual fermentation aromas. Also, if we value these things, leave more space, I guess. Advice for the future. Make estery beers with all sorts of applicable head-spaces so as to maximize interesting beers. Here's hoping that sticks, somewhere. What that means for now is that the beer will ferment, hopefully with adequate yeast presence. I already know what it might end up like with no real hop presence so I'm taking that as my baseline. Again, I hope that some honey and some proven American hops can create the basis of something special with mixed with truly remarkably Norwegian yeast.

08/16/2018 10:01am

It's going pretty strongly now. Most of the head space is filled up. Hopefully it has expanded as much as it will need to, because there isn't much more room for it to go.

08/16/2018 11:05am

Good thing I checked it again, because airlock infiltration had begun. After some scrambling, I swapped in the tube and bucket. The crazy thing about this yeast is that the carboy heated up a lot in the last hour. Suddenly, the glass even feels warmer to the touch and, obviously, fermentation has literally gone through the roof. That said, all of this will probably only last a few more hours.

08/17/2018 10:37pm

Fermentation had slowed to a crawl earlier this morning, so I put the airlock back in. With the decline in activity came a drop in temperature, so I have wrapped the carboy in a blanket again (whereas when it was really cranking I actually took it off to keep the whole thing from overheating too much). Just like that, most of the fermentation is complete, but I'll still give it about two more weeks to finish up.
